15. The Prince and the Magician's Bride

  In this story, a prince named Ratnabhanu fell in love with a beautiful girl named Ratnavati. Ratnavati was the daughter of a magician who had a magical flying horse. The magician agreed to give his daughter's hand in marriage to the prince, but he had a condition: the prince had to bring him a magical tree that bore golden fruits.

  The prince accepted the challenge and set out on his quest. After facing many hardships and dangers, he found the magical tree and plucked a golden fruit. However, on his way back, he encountered a demon who wanted the fruit for himself. After a fierce battle, the prince managed to defeat the demon and return to the magician's palace.

  When the magician learned about the demon and the prince's bravery, he agreed to marry Ratnavati to the prince. The prince and Ratnavati got married and lived happily. However, their happiness was short-lived because the magician was secretly planning to use Ratnavati's life force to make himself immortal.

  Ratnavati discovered her father's evil intentions and sought the help of a wise sage. The sage devised a plan to thwart the magician's scheme. He instructed Ratnavati to pretend to be ill and convince her father that only the flesh of a lion that had killed a man could cure her.

  The magician, eager to save his daughter, went on a hunt and was eventually killed by a lion. Ratnavati's life was saved, and she and the prince lived happily ever after.

  When Betal finished narrating this story, he posed a question to King Vikramaditya: "What could the magician have done differently to avoid his downfall?"

  Vikramaditya thought for a moment and replied, "The magician should have valued his daughter's happiness and well-being over his desire for immortality. Instead of resorting to evil means, he could have supported his daughter's marriage and found fulfillment in her happiness."

  Betal, impressed by Vikramaditya's answer, flew back to the tree, and the cycle of stories continued.
