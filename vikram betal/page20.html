20. The Three Thieves

  

  In this story, there were three thieves named Harichand, Govind, and Keshav. One day, they planned to rob a wealthy merchant who lived in a nearby town. They devised a plan to enter the merchant's house at night and steal his treasures.

  As the thieves sneaked into the merchant's house, they accidentally made a noise, waking the merchant. The merchant quickly realized that thieves were in his house, so he pretended to be asleep and overheard their conversation. The thieves, not noticing that the merchant was awake, began to argue.

  Harichand said, "Let's divide the loot equally among us."

  Govind disagreed, saying, "I am the one who found this house, so I should get a bigger share."

  Keshav chimed in, "No, I should get the largest share because I am the one who picked the lock to enter the house."

  Their argument escalated, and they decided to go to a wise man in their village to resolve the matter. This wise man was none other than King Vikramaditya himself, in disguise.

  When they presented their case to the wise man, he listened carefully and then said, "I will divide the loot for you." He gave each thief one blow and said, "This blow is your share. You can divide it equally among yourselves if you wish."

  The thieves were shocked but realized that the wise man had cleverly shown them the folly of their ways. They left the wise man's place with just one blow each, understanding the importance of unity and cooperation.

  As Vikramaditya was carrying Betal on his shoulders, Betal praised the king's wisdom and asked him, "What lesson can be learned from this story?"

  Vikramaditya replied, "The story teaches us that unity and cooperation lead to success. When people work together harmoniously, they can achieve great things, but greed and discord can lead to their downfall."

  Impressed by Vikramaditya's insight, Betal flew back to the tree, and the cycle of stories continued.
